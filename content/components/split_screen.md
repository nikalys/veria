# Split Screen Layout

**Definition**: A distinct macro-layout that divides the user's viewport perfectly down the middle (typically a 50/50 vertical split), creating two equal, side-by-side content panels. It is used to present dual importance or a strong juxtaposition between copy (text) and imagery.

**History**: One of the earliest layout responses to the wide, high-resolution monitors of the 2010s that replaced the ubiquitous 1024x768 fixed-width container. As designers realized stretching reading text across a massive monitor was bad for legibility, they simply broke the layout in half: one half constrained reading width, the other half massive imagery. Apple significantly popularized the layout for its Mac and iPad landing pages.

**Variations**:
- **50/50 Classic**: The true split. Text on the left, full-bleed imagery on the right (or vice versa). Extremely structured and easy to read.
- **Asymmetrical Split (30/70 or 40/60)**: Often used when the textual content is minimal, allowing the visual element to take absolute precedence without totally subsuming the text.
- **Sticky / Scrolling Split**: One side (usually the text or navigation) remains fixed, while the other side (usually imagery or varied content blocks) scrolls independently. Highly immersive.
- **Split Navigation**: Instead of a horizontal top bar, the navigation itself forms one half of the screen, acting as a massive, permanent directory beside the actively viewed page.

**Use Cases**:
- **SaaS Sign-in / Onboarding Screens**: The absolute standard pattern. The login form sits cleanly on a white background on the left, while a marketing illustration or "social proof" quote sits on a colorful background on the right.
- **E-Commerce Product Details**: Allowing the user to scroll through multiple high-res product photos on one side while the "Add to Cart" block remains sticky on the other.
- **Agency Portfolios / Editorials**: Creating dramatic juxtaposition. A massive, beautiful photo on one side, and stark, minimalist Swiss typography describing it on the other.
- **Dual Audience Landing Pages**: E.g., "I'm a Buyer" on the left panel, "I'm a Seller" on the right.
