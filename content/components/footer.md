# Footer

**Definition**: A distinct, persistent structural container located at the absolute bottom of a webpage. Unlike the top Navigation Bar (which highlights primary routing), the footer acts as the website's administrative anchor, housing secondary links, legal information, copyright notices, social media icons, language selectors, and newsletter signups.

**History**: In the 1990s, footers were tiny, consisting solely of a copyright symbol and a Webmaster email. As the web matured and sites ballooned to hundreds of pages, the "Fat Footer" paradigm emerged. Designers realized that when a user reaches the bottom of a page, they are often lost or looking for specific, un-marketable information (like "Return Policy" or "Careers"). The footer expanded to become a comprehensive "sitemap" fallback.

**Variations**:
- **The "Fat" Footer / Sitemap**: The modern enterprise standard. Contains 3 to 5 vertical columns of categorized links outlining the entire architecture of the website, a newsletter input field, and a massive branding block.
- **Simple / Minimalist Footer**: Often used by minimalist portfolios or single-purpose landing pages. Just a copyright notice, a Twitter link, and a "Privacy Policy" link sitting on a single centered line.
- **Contextual / Sticky Footer**: A small footer that is fixed to the bottom of the viewport at all times (often used in web apps or on mobile devices to house persistent music controls or help buttons).
- **The Infinite Scroll Problem**: If a site uses Infinite Scroll, the footer is effectively unreachable (as more content keeps loading every time you try to scroll to the bottom). This forces designers to either use the Hybrid "Load More" button or stick the footer information into a permanent left/right sidebar.

**Use Cases**:
- **Global Site Navigation Fallback**: If a user cannot find what they want in the main menu, they invariably scroll immediately to the bottom of the site to check the footer columns.
- **SEO Optimization**: Linking to deeply buried administrative, legal, or geographic pages (e.g., "Locations in Texas", "Locations in California") that wouldn't make sense in the main marketing flow.
- **Trust & Legal Compliance**: The universal location for "Terms of Service", "Privacy Policy", and "Cookie Consent" links.
