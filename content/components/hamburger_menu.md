# Hamburger / Off-Canvas Menu

**Definition**: A space-saving navigation pattern consisting of an icon (three horizontal lines, resembling a hamburger) that, when tapped or clicked, triggers a hidden navigation panel to slide into view from off-screen (usually the left or right edge), pushing or covering the main content.

**History**: Designed by Norm Cox in 1981 for the Xerox Star workstation, the "hamburger" icon sat dormant for decades until the smartphone explosion (post-2007). As designers struggled to fit massive horizontal web navbars onto 320px-wide iPhone screens, the Hamburger menu became the absolute, undisputed standard for mobile web navigation. Its use on desktop sites remains controversial but prevalent.

**Variations**:
- **Slide-In Drawer (Over)**: The menu slides in from the left or right, covering a portion of the main screen with a drop-shadow. The background content is usually dimmed (an overlay) to prevent interaction.
- **Push Drawer**: The menu slides in from the edge and physically pushes the main content block out of the way to the side.
- **Full-Screen Takeover**: Clicking the hamburger icon causes a solid-colored screen to fade or slide up, completely covering the viewport and displaying massive, centralized text links. Highly popular in premium or brutalist design.
- **Bottom Sheet**: A modern mobile variant where the menu slides up from the bottom of the screen instead of the side, making it significantly easier to reach with a thumb on massive phones.

**Use Cases**:
- **Mobile Web & Responsive Design**: The absolute necessity for taking a desktop Navbar and collapsing it for mobile users.
- **Minimalist Portfolios**: Desktop sites that want the main interface to be a single massive photo or video will hide the standard navigation behind a hamburger to maintain artistic purity.
- **Secondary App Actions**: In complex SaaS apps, primary navigation might live in a sidebar, but a right-side hamburger menu might open a "settings" or "notifications" off-canvas drawer.
