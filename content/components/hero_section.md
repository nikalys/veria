# Hero Section

**Definition**: The first, massive visual element a user sees when landing on a website. It is typically a full-width container sitting immediately below the navigation bar (or encompassing it) and contains a combination of a striking background (image, video, or color), a primary headline, a sub-headline, and a Call to Action (CTA).

**History**: The term originates from print publishing ("hero image" being the largest, most important photo on a page). On the web, it became ubiquitous in the early 2010s as responsive web design forced designers to abandon dense, multi-column homepages in favor of single-column scrolling experiences. The Hero Section became the digital storefront window, meant to hook the user in the first 3 seconds before they scroll.

**Variations**:
- **Centered Hero**: The text (headline, subhead, CTA) is center-aligned over a background image, gradient, or solid color. Very common, very safe, but can have text-readability issues if the background isn't darkened.
- **Split-Screen Hero (50/50)**: The viewport is divided vertically. One side holds the text and CTA on a solid background; the other holds the "hero" visual (illustration, photo, or 3D render). Solves readability issues entirely.
- **Full-Bleed Hero**: The background image or video stretches edge-to-edge covering the entire viewport (100vh).
- **Typographic Hero**: Rejects imagery entirely in favor of massive, screen-filling typography. Very popular in agency portfolios and minimalist SaaS tools.
- **Video Background Hero**: Instead of a static image, a looping, muted video plays in the background to instantly convey atmosphere or product usage.

**Use Cases**:
- **SaaS Landing Pages**: The split-screen variant is the absolute standard for B2B software, allowing clear value propositions alongside a product dashboard screenshot.
- **E-commerce Homepages**: Used to highlight the newest seasonal collection or a massive promotional sale.
- **Event Sites**: A full-bleed video hero is often used to convey the energy and scale of a festival or conference immediately.
- **Personal Portfolios**: Typographic heroes are frequently used by designers to make a bold, immediate statement about their skills.
