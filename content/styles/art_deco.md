# Art Deco

**Definition**: An aesthetic of geometric luxury and structured symmetry, characterized by rich colors (frequently deep black, emerald green, or navy blue), metallic accents (predominantly gold, brass, or chrome), sharp, repeating geometric patterns (chevrons, zigzags, sunbursts), and extremely elegant, elongated sans-serif or stylized serif typography.

**History**: The preeminent visual arts design style of the 1920s and 1930s (originating in Paris before World War I). It represented modernism turned into high fashion—sleek, anti-traditional, and symbolizing wealth and technological progress (e.g., the Chrysler Building). In digital UI, it experienced a massive resurgence in the 2010s ("The Roaring 2020s") as brands sought a way to communicate established luxury without resorting to stuffy, classical minimalism or harsh modernism.

**Variations**:
- **The Gatsby (Roaring 20s)**: The most recognizable implementation. Deep black backgrounds with hyper-thin, intricate gold geometric framing and borders. Highly symmetrical, centering logos and text like physical invitations.
- **Miami Deco (Tropical Deco)**: A lighter, almost pastel subgenre (think South Beach architectures). Less black/gold, more mint green, flamingo pink, and stark white, while retaining the strict geometric symmetry and stepped arches.
- **Streamline Moderne**: The later phase of Deco focusing on aerodynamic curves, long horizontal lines, and nautical/industrial shapes rather than sharp chevrons.

**Use Cases**:
- **High-End Hospitality & Dining**: Unquestionably the most dominant aesthetic for luxury boutique hotels, cocktail bars, and Michelin-star restaurant websites.
- **Real Estate & Architecture**: Elite property developments use Deco to signal historic permanence and extreme wealth.
- **Boutique Agencies**: Used by high-end design or branding agencies to differentiate themselves from the standard "Clean Functional SaaS" aesthetic.
