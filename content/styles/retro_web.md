# Retro / Vintage Web (Web 1.0)

**Definition**: A nostalgic aesthetic that intentionally mimics the severe technical limitations of the early internet (1994–1999). Characterized by bright, clashing web-safe colors (pure blue links, purple visited links), heavy use of tiled background images, pixelated (dithered) graphics, default Times New Roman typography, visible table borders, and chaotic animations like `<marquee>` tags and rotating 3D GIFs.

**History**: The "Vintage Web" was simply the reality of building sites on dial-up connections before CSS existed. Layouts were hacked together using HTML tables, and images were heavily compressed (dithered) to load quickly. As a modern design aesthetic, it re-emerged in the late 2010s as internet culture aged enough to feel nostalgia for its chaotic, lawless, and highly personal beginnings—acting as a rebellion against the homogenized, template-driven "modern" SaaS web.

**Variations**:
- **Geocities / Angelfire Maximalism**: The chaotic teenage bedroom of the internet. Auto-playing MIDI files, under-construction GIFs, visitor counters, and star-field backgrounds.
- **Academic / Terminal Web**: Pure text, default gray backgrounds (`#C0C0C0`), horizontal rule lines `<hr>`, and standard blue anchor tags. Feels like a 1995 university server directory.
- **Mac OS System 7 / Windows 95 UI**: Designing the website to look exactly like an early desktop operating system window, complete with pixelated close buttons, gray bevels, and Chicago or MS Sans Serif fonts.
- **Vaporwave Web**: Taking the 90s web elements (marble statues, early 3D renders, Windows 95 dialog boxes) and applying a surreal, neon-pastel Los Angeles color palette.

**Use Cases**:
- **Personal Sites & Developer Portfolios**: A popular way for engineers to show personality, humor, and a deep appreciation for internet history.
- **Underground Music & Streetwear**: Used to signal authenticity, anti-commercialism, and a DIY (Do-It-Yourself) punk ethic.
- **Marketing Stunts**: Major brands (like Spotify or Nike) occasionally releasing a "Web 1.0" version of a campaign to manufacture viral nostalgia.
- **Digital Archives & Museums**: Designing the wrapper of the site to match the era of the content being displayed.
