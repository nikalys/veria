# Minimalism (Web)

**Definition**: A design philosophy defined by the removal of non-essential elements, content, and features. It relies on extreme whitespace (negative space), highly limited color palettes (often monochrome), strong but sparse typography, and a massive signal-to-noise ratio. It is defined as much by what is missing as by what is present.

**History**: Rooted in early 20th-century architecture and art (Mies van der Rohe's "less is more" and the De Stijl movement), it translated to the web as early as the late 90s but became the dominant luxury and modern standard in the 2010s. With the rise of high-resolution displays, designers realized they didn't need heavy borders or textures to separate content—whitespace alone was enough.

**Variations**:
- **Black & White / Monochromatic Minimalism**: Relies entirely on stark contrast, typography scale, and grid alignment, often stripping away imagery entirely in favor of huge type.
- **Editorial Minimalism**: High-end fashion or photography portfolios that mix negative space with perfectly framed, high-quality bespoke imagery.
- **Utilitarian / Functional Minimalism**: The "Craigslist" or simple SaaS approach—ugly to some, but highly effective because it only presents the absolute minimum required data without aesthetic flair.
- **Warm / Organic Minimalism**: A recent trend mixing minimalism with earthy, natural color palettes (beige, sage, taupe) and softer photography, moving away from stark sterile whites.

**Use Cases**:
- **Luxury Brand E-commerce**: Watches, fashion, and high-end automotive brands use minimalism to force focus strictly on the product photography.
- **Creative Portfolios**: Agencies and artists use it so the interface doesn't compete with the work being showcased.
- **High-Focus Applications**: Writing apps (like iA Writer) or focus tools where absolute distraction-free environments are mandatory.
- **Search & Utility**: The classic Google homepage is the ultimate functional minimalist interface.
