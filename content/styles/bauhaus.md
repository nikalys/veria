# Bauhaus

**Definition**: A highly influential design philosophy emphasizing functionalism, strict geometric forms (circles, squares, triangles), primary colors (red, blue, yellow) alongside black and white, and asymmetrical balance. Above all, it abides by the mantra "form follows function"—meaning every aesthetic choice must serve a utilitarian purpose.

**History**: Founded by Walter Gropius in 1919 in Weimar, Germany, the Bauhaus school sought to combine fine art with craft and mass production. Although closed by the Nazi regime in 1933, its teachers scattered globally, making its philosophy the bedrock of modern industrial, architectural, and graphic design. On the web, it heavily influenced early flat design and the overarching discipline of building robust css grid systems.

**Variations**:
- **Classical Bauhaus**: Strict adherence to primary colors, dense asymmetrical typography, and raw geometric shapes used to guide the eye or segment content.
- **Modernist Grid**: Less focused on the primary colors, more focused on the strict, visible grid systems and functional hierarchy. The "container-first" aesthetic seen in high-end brutalism.
- **De Stijl (The Style)**: A closely related Dutch movement (Piet Mondrian) focusing entirely on pure abstraction and universality through a reduction to the essentials of form and colour: vertical and horizontal lines, and primary colors.
- **Industrial Bauhaus**: A heavier, more mechanical implementation. Monospaced typography, stark black backgrounds, thick dividers, prioritizing raw mechanical utility over visual comfort.

**Use Cases**:
- **Architecture and Industrial Design Portfolios**: The natural home for the aesthetic, demonstrating an understanding of core design history and grid-systems.
- **High-End E-commerce**: Some fashion or furniture brands use Bauhaus principles (strict grids, stark contrast) to present a highly structured, premium shopping experience.
- **Typography and Foundries**: Font foundries often use Bauhaus presentation to showcase geometric sans-serif typefaces (like Futura or its modern derivatives).
- **Museums & Art Institutions**: Digital exhibitions frequently employ the stark colors and asymmetrical layouts of the movement.
